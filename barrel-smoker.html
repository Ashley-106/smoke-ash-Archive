Barrel Smoker

Before

After

Step 1: Burn to remove paint and contaminants

Firstly clean the barrel, the easiest way to do this is to burn the old paint work and anything left in the barrel off. This will give us a clean surface to work with.

Step 2: Clean and paint

After burning the barrel you will be left with loose paint flakes and ash, this needs to be cleaned. A layer of paint can be added to prevent the smoker from rusting.The paint will need to be heat resistant.

Step 3: Add vent and handles

The smoke produced from the charcoal will need to be exhausted out of the lid of the smoker, a perfect way of doing this is with an adjustable BBQ smoke vent. You can also add handles for ease of use when removing the lid.

Step 4: Add air flow pipes

The smoker will need good air circulation to allow the charcoal to continue burning. This can be achieved with an vent pipe attached to either side. An inline valve will allow you to adjust the amount of airflow, thereby controlling the heat within the smoker.

    What you will need:
  • A single length of 20mm copper pipe (usually supplied 3m length)
  • 2x 20mm inline-valves
  • 2x 20mm right angle connections
  • 4x 20mm clips

Step 5: Add Temp gage and base for grill

Add a BBQ temperature gage roughly 3 inches down from the top of the smokers lid. This is the level the meat will be cooking at and you will want to know what temperature this is so you can regulate the exhaust and air flow valves. Add a band of self tapping screws at roughly 6 inches down from the top of the smokers lid this will be the base to sit your grill onto. Length of screws depends on diameter of grill.
